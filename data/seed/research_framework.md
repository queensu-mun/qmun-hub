# Research Framework for MUN Country Prep

How to actually research a country position — sources, process, and what to prioritize when you have limited time.

## The three questions (Queen's team framework)

Every brief is built around three questions:

1. How does your country feel about this issue? What domestic actions have they taken?
2. How does the international community feel? What past international actions exist?
3. What is your strategy in committee? What do you want to pass?

This is not just a writing framework. It is a research checklist. You are not done researching until you can answer all three with specifics.

## Where to research

### For country positions (Question 1)

**UN Bibliographic Information System (unbisnet.un.org)**: Search your country + topic. Gives you actual voting records, statements in committee, and co-sponsorship history. The gold standard. Most delegates don't use it; that's your edge.

**Country's foreign ministry website**: Look for press releases and official statements. Governments publish their positions openly. Search `site:[ministry domain] [topic]`.

**UN General Debate statements**: Every country's head of state or foreign minister gives a statement at the General Debate each September. They announce their priorities. Search "[country] General Debate statement [year]" on un.org.

**UNGA voting record**: Research the UN Digital Library (digitallibrary.un.org). Filter by your country. Look at how they voted on resolutions related to your topic in the last 5 years.

### For international context (Question 2)

**UN Treaty Collection (treaties.un.org)**: Which treaties and conventions exist on this topic? Which states have signed and ratified? Your country's ratification status tells you what they're legally committed to.

**Past resolutions**: Search the UN Digital Library for resolutions on your topic. Read the preambular clauses — they summarize the negotiating history. Read the operative clauses — they tell you what the international community has already agreed to and what's still contested.

**UN Special Rapporteurs and expert bodies**: For human rights topics, the OHCHR website has country reports. For development topics, UNDP country profiles. For security topics, Security Council meeting records.

**Academic sources (Google Scholar)**: For background understanding. Don't cite academic papers in committee — it looks weak. Use them to understand the issue, then cite the UN primary sources.

### For strategy (Question 3)

**Other delegations' past positions**: If you can find position papers from previous conferences on the same topic, read what countries with similar interests have proposed. The UN Digital Library and conference websites often post these.

**Bloc alignment**: WHO else has the same interests as you? Start with your regional group (G77, EU, African Group, etc.) and then identify the swing votes. The swing votes are where you spend your unmod time.

## Time allocation for a weekend conference

**If you have one week:** Spend two days on each question, one day revising and building your strategy.

**If you have 48 hours:** Two hours on Question 1 (country position), two hours on Question 2 (international context), one hour on Question 3 (strategy and talking points). In that order.

**If you have the night before:** One hour, Question 1 only. Know your country's position and two specific past actions. You can wing Questions 2 and 3 in committee if you understand your own country.

## What "knowing your country" actually means

Weak prep: "France supports climate action and human rights."

Strong prep: "France co-sponsored Resolution 75/219 on climate and security in 2020, abstained on 76/300 citing sovereignty concerns, and has been pushing for an advisory opinion from the ICJ since 2022. Their position is shaped by their permanent seat ambitions and their overseas territory exposure to sea-level rise."

The difference is specifics. Chairs can tell. The delegate who can cite a resolution number and explain the abstention is the delegate who wins.

## Common research mistakes

**Reading Wikipedia first.** Fine for background. Terrible as a primary source. Chairs know when you're working from Wikipedia — the positions are generic and the history is shallow.

**Skipping the voting record.** Countries say one thing and vote another. The vote is the truth. A country that "supports the environment" but consistently votes against binding enforcement mechanisms is not actually your ally.

**Not reading what the bloc has already agreed to.** If you propose a clause that contradicts a resolution your country co-sponsored, you will be called out. Read the history.

**Over-researching and under-strategizing.** Some delegates read for 30 hours and still don't know what they want to pass. Question 3 is where conferences are won. Spend real time on it.
