# Writing Resolutions

A resolution is the final output of a committee session. It is the document your working paper becomes if it passes. Understanding how resolutions are structured tells you how to write clauses that actually advance your bloc's position.

## The two-part structure

Every resolution has the same architecture: preambulatory clauses first, operative clauses second.

**Preambulatory clauses** establish context. They explain why the committee is acting. They cite precedent, acknowledge prior resolutions, and set up the problem your operative clauses will address. They do not direct anyone to do anything.

**Operative clauses** direct action. They are numbered. They tell member states, the Secretary-General, or subsidiary bodies what to do. These are the clauses that matter. Everything in a resolution is in service of the operative clauses.

## Preambulatory phrases

Each preambulatory clause opens with a present participle. Common ones:

- **Affirming** — strong assertion of a principle or value
- **Alarmed by** — signals urgency; use when the problem is acute
- **Acknowledging** — neutral recognition of a fact or condition
- **Bearing in mind** — invokes background context
- **Deeply concerned** — signals priority without committing to a specific cause
- **Emphasizing** — underscores a principle you will build on operatively
- **Noting** — neutral; good for citing statistics or prior documents
- **Recalling** — references a prior resolution or treaty; establishes precedent
- **Recognizing** — similar to acknowledging, slightly warmer

Do not write preambulatory clauses that say nothing. "Recognizing that climate change is a global challenge" is filler. "Recognizing that the 2022 IPCC report found a 1.5°C pathway requires 45% emissions cuts by 2030" is substantive.

## Operative phrases

Each operative clause opens with a present tense verb. The verb carries legal weight — choose carefully.

**Strong directives:**
- **Calls upon** — urges action; not binding but the strongest non-mandatory language
- **Condemns** — the strongest censure language; hard to pass in divided committees
- **Demands** — binding in Security Council resolutions; aggressive in GA contexts
- **Decides** — makes a formal decision binding on the UN body itself (budgets, subsidiary bodies)
- **Establishes** — creates a new mechanism, body, or fund
- **Urges** — slightly weaker than Calls upon; often used for non-member state actors

**Medium directives:**
- **Encourages** — soft; appropriate for areas where consensus is fragile
- **Recommends** — suggests action without requiring it
- **Requests** — formal ask to the SG, a UN body, or member states
- **Reaffirms** — recaps a prior commitment; good for preamble-like operatives

**Weaker directives (avoid overusing):**
- **Notes** — operational noting; only use when you want the record to reflect something without directing action
- **Invites** — very soft; used for external actors like NGOs

## Sub-clauses

Any operative clause can have sub-clauses (a), (b), (c)... that elaborate. Use them when a directive has multiple components. A well-structured operative clause with sub-clauses reads like a brief policy:

> 3. *Encourages* member states to develop national adaptation plans, including:
>    (a) multi-hazard early warning systems,
>    (b) community-level resilience funds for coastal populations,
>    (c) annual progress reporting to the UNFCCC;

## What makes a clause weak

- **Vague actors**: "Calls upon nations to act" — which nations? On what timeline? With what authority?
- **Restating the problem**: preambulatory language hiding in operative clauses
- **Toothless mechanisms**: "Encourages the creation of a voluntary fund" — voluntary and encourages in the same clause means nothing happens
- **Redundant sub-clauses**: adding (a), (b), (c) that say the same thing three ways

## The amendment process

Friendly amendments have support from the original sponsors and get folded in without a vote. Unfriendly amendments require a vote and are the main tool for weakening a paper you oppose.

When you draft, anticipate which clauses are most vulnerable to unfriendly amendment. Clauses with binding language in General Assembly contexts ("demands" instead of "calls upon") are easy targets. Specific funding numbers are easy to amend out. Consider putting your most important substance in the sub-clauses — they are harder to target without amending the whole operative.

## Common mistakes

**Too many preambulatory clauses.** Three to five is enough. Twelve preambulatory clauses signals that the drafters padded the paper to look substantial. Chairs notice.

**Operative clauses that preambulate.** An operative clause that begins "Recognizing that climate adaptation requires" is a preambulatory clause in the wrong section.

**Missing operative specificity.** "Calls upon member states to address food insecurity" is not a resolution clause. It is a goal. A resolution clause specifies: who does what, with what mechanism, on what timeline, reporting to whom.

**Working paper language vs resolution language.** Working papers are drafts. When you table, your paper becomes a draft resolution. Write it in final-form language from the start — present tense verbs, no "proposed operative clause 3."
