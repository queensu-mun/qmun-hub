# Crisis Directives: What Works and What Gets Ignored

Crisis committee is a different game from GA. In GA, you win with coalition-building and operative clauses. In crisis, you win with directives — written orders your character sends to agents, organizations, or the world to take action. The difference between delegates who get awards and delegates who don't is almost entirely in how they write directives.

## What a directive is

A directive is a written instruction from your character (a head of state, minister, military commander, CEO, historical figure — whoever you're playing) to someone or something to do something specific.

The backroom staff (the crisis team) reads your directive, decides if it's feasible, and writes back with results. Good directives get results. Vague directives get ignored or get "your agent is working on it" as a non-answer.

## The anatomy of a good directive

**To:** Name the recipient. Specific names, departments, or organizations — not "my advisors." If your character controls the Ministry of Finance, name the Minister of Finance. If you have a spy network, name the network or its lead handler.

**Action:** One clear verb. "Seize," "transfer," "eliminate," "publish," "negotiate," "monitor," "destroy." Not "look into" or "deal with" or "handle." Vague verbs get vague results.

**Target:** What, specifically. Name names. Cite locations. Reference real things in the crisis world. "The eastern pipeline" not "critical infrastructure."

**Resources authorized:** What are you willing to commit. Money, personnel, political capital, legal authority. If you don't specify, backroom assumes you're authorizing minimal resources and the result reflects it.

**Timeline:** By when. "Immediately," "within 24 hours," "before the vote," "at the end of the next session." This matters because backroom is tracking the crisis arc.

**Success condition:** How will you know it worked. This is optional but it signals to backroom that you're thinking, and it gives them a natural resolution to report back.

## Example: weak vs. strong directive

**Weak:**
> To: My intelligence network
> Please gather information on what Russia is doing in the region and report back.

Backroom reads this and writes: "Your network is gathering intelligence." Nothing happens.

**Strong:**
> To: Director Chen, SIGINT Division
> Action: Intercept and decrypt communications between General Kozlov's 5th Army command and Moscow over the next 6 hours. Specifically: any orders regarding troop movements south of the Dnipro. Authorized: full Division resources, no civilian exposure.
> Success condition: Raw transcripts on my desk before the emergency session.

Backroom reads this and has something to work with. They write back with actual intelligence — or a reason it failed (jammed communications, Chen was compromised) that you now have to respond to. Either way, the story moves.

## Targeting your directives

The best crisis players think in terms of leverage, not just action. Before writing a directive, ask:

- **What do I control that others don't?** Information, money, legal authority, relationships, military assets. Your directives should use the unique assets of your character, not generic actions anyone could take.
- **What is the backroom most likely to say yes to?** Backroom is constrained by the crisis arc. Flashy directives that try to end the crisis in one move ("nuke the capital") get blocked. Incremental directives that build your position get results.
- **What information am I missing that would change my strategy?** The best first directive in any crisis is often an intelligence-gathering directive. Know the room before you act in it.

## Personal vs. press directives

**Personal directives** go to your agents and stay private. The committee doesn't see them unless backroom injects them into the public record (e.g., "leaked documents show that...").

**Press directives** are public statements your character makes. They shape how other delegates perceive your position. Useful for: claiming credit, shifting blame, rallying allies, creating public pressure.

Use both. A delegate who only sends personal directives is invisible in committee. A delegate who only sends press directives has no actual power behind their words.

## Responding to crisis updates

When backroom injects a crisis update, the question is: what does this change?

- If it helps you: acknowledge it publicly in committee, build on it, deepen your position.
- If it hurts you: respond immediately with a counter-directive before other delegates can use it against you. First-mover advantage in crisis is real.
- If it seems unrelated: it isn't. Backroom plants everything deliberately. Ask what the second-order effect is.

## The most common crisis mistakes at Queen's

**Too passive.** "I will wait and see what happens" is not a directive. Waiting is a losing strategy unless you're specifically gathering intelligence to inform a larger move. Passive delegates are invisible to chairs.

**Too vague.** "Deal with the economic situation" tells backroom nothing. Write specific verbs, specific targets, specific resources. If you're not sure what to order, order intelligence-gathering first.

**Treating crisis like a GA debate.** You're not trying to pass a resolution. You're trying to advance your character's interests in a dynamic world. The committee "solving" the crisis is not the point — your character shaping how it gets solved is.

**Ignoring what other delegates are doing.** Other players are also sending directives. Their actions affect your situation. Read the crisis updates carefully for signs of what other characters are doing, and respond to it.

**Writing the same directive twice.** If backroom said no or gave you a partial result, sending the same directive again signals you're not adapting. Adjust the approach, change the resources, find a different angle.
