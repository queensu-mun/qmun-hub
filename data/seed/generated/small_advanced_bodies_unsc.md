# Small Advanced Bodies: UNSC and Other Elite Rooms

How to compete in 15-seat rooms where everyone is good, the dais sees everything, and the veto changes the entire negotiation. Built on our NCSC UNSC experience.

## What makes these rooms different

A Security Council simulation, an Ad Hoc committee, or any 10 to 20 seat advanced body is a different sport from GA. Three structural changes drive everything:

1. Total visibility. The dais hears every word you say all weekend. There is no hiding and no coasting session. One lazy intervention is remembered.
2. Everyone is strong. Conferences staff these rooms with experienced applicants and schools send their anchors. The baseline is high, so awards are won on margins: preparation depth, negotiation craft, composure.
3. Power asymmetry is real. In the UNSC, the P5 veto means a resolution is not a numbers game. Ten votes mean nothing against one angry permanent member. The entire weekend is a negotiation with the P5 in a way GA delegates are never forced to learn.

These rooms also pay disproportionately. At NCSC the UNSC is double-delegation with awards counted twice toward delegation totals, and advanced committees carry an extra weighting. The seat is expensive in prep time and worth it.

## Preparation standard

Half-prepared delegates are exposed within an hour in a small room. The bar:

- Know your country's actual Security Council record on the topic: real votes, real abstentions, real vetoes, and the stated reasoning. Quoting your own country's past UNSC statements is devastating credibility in this room.
- Know the institutional toolbox precisely. Chapter VI versus Chapter VII, what a presidential statement is versus a resolution, how sanctions committees and panels of experts actually work, what a peacekeeping mandate renewal looks like. Advanced daises reward delegates who reach for the right instrument.
- Map the other 14 seats before you arrive. For each country: core interest on the topic, red lines, likely patron or client relationships. In a 15-seat room you can genuinely model every actor, so do it.

## Playing your seat honestly

If you hold a P5 seat, your power is the threat, not the use. Veto threats spend credibility every time you make one, and an actual veto should feel like a controlled detonation you have prepared the room for. P5 delegates who veto casually read as unserious.

If you hold an elected seat, your power is brokerage. The E10 delegate who wins the room is the one who finds the language bridge between two P5 positions. You cannot out-muscle the P5, so out-author them: hold the pen on the draft, chair the informal huddles, become the delegate both sides route through.

## The rhythm of a small room

Formal and informal blur. Expect long consultations, rolling unmods, and drafting huddles around one laptop. Some practical craft:

- Hold the pen. Same law as the big GA but stronger: in a one-draft room, the penholder is the protagonist. Volunteer immediately.
- Negotiate in fragments. Advanced rooms move clause by clause. Win the weekend by winning specific operative paragraphs: a mandate line, a sanctions carve-out, a reporting requirement. Track which clauses are yours.
- Use precision as a weapon. When debate gets vague, the delegate who says "the disagreement is just the word 'demands' in OP4, here are three alternatives" takes control of the room.
- Manage crisis updates without panic. Many UNSC sims inject crisis breaks. The test is triage: respond to the update without abandoning the resolution thread, because the dais is checking whether you can hold both.

## Double-delegation in advanced rooms

When the seat is a pair (as at NCSC's UNSC), the partnership is the performance. Split roles cleanly: one partner owns the floor and the formal record, the other owns the informal negotiation and drafting, and rotate enough that the dais sees both of you as essential. See the double-delegation guide for the full playbook; in an advanced room the cardinal sin is visible disagreement between partners, because everyone notices.

## From verbal commendation to gavel

Our 2025 NCSC result proves we belong in the room. The gap between a verbal and a Best Delegate in an advanced body is rarely talent. It is usually one of three things: prep depth (the winner knew the sanctions regime by resolution number), pen control (the winner held the draft from hour two), or arc patience (the winner saved their decisive move for Saturday instead of peaking Friday). Pick the anchor pair early, give them a month of seat-specific prep, run at least one full mock UNSC session against alumni or coaches playing hostile P5 members, and send them in with a clause-level plan.
