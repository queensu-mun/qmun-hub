# Conference Dossier: NCSC (National Collegiate Security Conference)

Strategy brief for Georgetown's NCSC: what the conference rewards, how its crisis-heavy format shapes the weekend, and how Queen's should prepare. Our 2025 UNSC verbal commendation is the baseline to build on.

## The basics

NCSC is hosted by the Georgetown International Relations Association in Washington, DC, and has run since 1973, which makes it one of the oldest collegiate conferences on the circuit. It draws roughly 700 delegates over four days, typically late October or early November, at a downtown DC hotel. The conference identity is security: realistic, modern simulations of cabinets, national assemblies, corporate boards, councils, and regional organizations. The internal norm is no committees set before WWII, and no fantasy committees. If you show up expecting a Hogwarts cabinet, you are at the wrong conference.

The committee split leans hard toward crisis. A typical year runs around 20 crisis simulations against only a handful of GA-style assemblies. This is the single most important fact for delegation planning: NCSC is a crisis conference, and our roster should be weighted toward delegates who can write fast directives and manage a backroom arc, not just give clean GA speeches.

## What NCSC rewards

NCSC publishes its awards math, which is rare and useful. Individual awards run Best Delegate, Outstanding, Honorable Mention, Verbal Commendation, scored 5-4-3-2 toward delegation awards. Two structural details matter for us:

1. Awards in double-delegation committees (the UNSC and the double-del specialized body) count twice toward delegation totals.
2. Advanced committees, meaning the Ad Hoc committees and the UNSC, carry a 1.25 weighting.

So a Best Delegate in the UNSC as a double delegation is worth dramatically more to the team score than the same award in a mid-size crisis room. Our 2025 verbal commendation in the UNSC tells us we can hang in that room. The next step is converting a verbal into an Outstanding or Best, because that is where the leverage lives. Put our strongest pair there every year and treat UNSC prep as its own program.

Delegation awards split Large (12 or more delegates by live attendance) and Small (11 or fewer, minimum 6 to qualify). Queen's typically travels small to NCSC. That is good news: Best Small Delegation is genuinely winnable for a focused 8 to 11 person team, and McGill has shown a Canadian school can take it. We are not competing against American University's 40-person machine in our bracket.

Award density is also published: crisis rooms expect 5 to 6 awards, assemblies 7 to 10 depending on size. In a 15-person crisis room, a third of the room places. Every seat we send should have a realistic award plan.

## Committee style and pacing

NCSC crisis rooms are fast and grounded. Directors reward plausibility: directives that respect real chains of command, real budgets, real institutional constraints. The classic NCSC failure mode is a delegate who treats the backroom like a video game and gets quietly frozen out by a director who wanted realism.

Pacing of the weekend: Thursday evening opening session sets first impressions and early bloc geography. Friday is the long grind, usually three sessions, and it is where arcs are built or die. Saturday is climax day, when the backroom pays off or does not, plus the social. Sunday morning is one short session and closing. Plan energy accordingly: the delegates who win are the ones still sharp in Saturday's third hour, not the ones who burned everything Thursday night trying to be loud.

DC adds texture: committees sometimes run site excursions and bring in practitioner guest speakers. Treat those as committee time, not breaks. Directors notice who asks the smart question to the guest.

## What strong delegations do

The teams that perform at NCSC (Georgetown's own circuit team, UChicago, McGill, Florida State in recent seasons) share habits we can copy:

- They scout the committee list the day it drops and match delegates to room types, not topics. A strong crisis delegate in the wrong room still produces; a GA specialist in an Ad Hoc room drowns.
- They run mock crisis sessions on the actual committee premise in the two weeks before. Not generic crisis practice, the actual scenario.
- They pre-write research, not directives. Directors smell canned directives. What you can pre-build is knowledge of the institutions, personalities, and resources your character controls.
- They treat the Ad Hoc seats as prestige seats and apply internally for them, because the secret topic format rewards pure skill and carries the 1.25 multiplier.

## Queen's plan, concretely

Target roster: 8 to 11 delegates so we sit in the Small bracket. Anchor pair in the UNSC. One or two delegates in advanced or Ad Hoc rooms if we win the application lottery. Everyone else in mid-size crisis committees where 5 to 6 awards drop.

Prep cadence: committee assignments mapped within 48 hours of release, two scenario-specific mock sessions, one session purely on directive writing under time pressure, and a team review of the awards math so everyone knows what their seat is worth. Goal for the next NCSC: at least one Outstanding or better, and a credible run at Outstanding Small Delegation.
