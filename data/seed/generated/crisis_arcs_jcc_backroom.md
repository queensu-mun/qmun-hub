# Crisis Arcs, Joint Crisis, and the Backroom Economy

How to plan a weekend-long crisis arc instead of writing one-off directives, how joint crisis committees change the game, and how to manage the backroom as an economy of scarce resources. Companion to the crisis directives guide, which covers directive writing itself.

## From directives to arcs

The directives guide teaches you to write a good order. This guide is about the layer above: the arc, meaning the connected sequence of moves that takes your character from their starting position to a position of decisive power by Saturday. Directors do not give Best Delegate for fifteen good unrelated directives. They give it for a story: the delegate whose moves in session one visibly set up their payoff in session five.

A working arc has three phases:

1. Foundation (sessions 1 and 2). Build capacity quietly. Secure income, recruit or create the people and organizations you will need, and establish information channels. Foundation directives are boring on purpose: a supply line, a hire, a survey, a shell company. Their job is to make later moves possible and plausible.
2. Development (the long middle). Convert capacity into position. Expand, form alliances in the room that mirror your backroom plans, and start shaping the crisis updates toward your strengths. This is also when you plant your cover stories.
3. Payoff (final sessions). Execute the decisive move your foundation made possible. The best payoffs are ones the director has watched you build for two days; they want to reward the delegate who made their crisis interesting.

Write the payoff first, in your prep, as a hypothesis: "by Saturday I want to be able to do X." Then derive the foundation backwards. You will adapt constantly, but delegates with a destination outplay delegates who only react.

## The backroom is an economy

Every crisis backroom runs on scarce resources, whether the staff frames it that way or not. The scarce things are:

- Staff attention. A backroom staffer is reading dozens of directives per session. Clear, skimmable, well-formatted directives get processed; rambling ones get skimmed and half-granted. Length is a cost, not a virtue.
- Plausibility budget. Each character gets a finite amount of "yes" for stretchy requests. Spend it on your arc's critical path, not on opportunistic side schemes.
- Your character's actual resources: money, personnel, authority, secrets. Track them in a ledger. Delegates who ask the backroom for things they never built get denied; delegates who write "using the engineering battalion I recruited in directive 4" get approved, because they are doing the staff's bookkeeping for them.

Practical habit: keep a running private document with three columns: assets I have built, directives in flight, and what I am owed. Reference earlier directives by number. Backroom staff reward delegates who make continuity easy.

## Frontroom and backroom must rhyme

The classic intermediate-delegate failure is running two disconnected games: cooperative statesman in the frontroom, unrelated schemer in the backroom. Directors compare notes between front and back staff, and incoherence reads as gimmickry. The strong version is integration: your public positions create cover and justification for your private moves, and your private moves manufacture the situations your public persona is positioned to solve. When a crisis update breaks and you are somehow the character with exactly the right assets in place, the room should be able to look back and see that you earned it in plain sight.

## Joint crisis committees

A JCC splits the simulation into two or more rooms (rival cabinets, opposing commands) acting against each other through a shared backroom. Everything above still applies, plus:

- The enemy is smart and human. Your opposing room is full of delegates doing exactly what you are doing. Expect your obvious moves to be anticipated, and value intelligence directives far more highly than in single-room crisis: knowing the other room's plans is the best asset in the game.
- Operational security matters. Assume leaks. Some JCCs allow or inject espionage between rooms; compartmentalize your real plan and consider feeding a fake one.
- In-room unity is a weapon. A cabinet that coordinates beats a cabinet of fifteen freelancers. Ironically, awards in JCCs often go to the delegate who organized their room's joint strategy, because directors see the difference it makes in the war.
- Tempo wins. In a two-room game, the side that acts first forces the other side to respond. Slow rooms spend the weekend reacting.

## Prep checklist for any crisis seat

- One-page character brief: powers, resources, relationships, enemies, and date-limited knowledge.
- A written arc hypothesis with a Saturday payoff and the three or four foundation moves it requires.
- Pre-built directive skeletons for the foundation moves (structure ready, details filled live; never fully canned).
- A resource ledger template ready to maintain from session one.
- For JCCs: a guess at the opposing room's three most likely strategies and one intelligence directive drafted for session one.
