# Specialized and Historical Committees

How to prepare for the rooms that are not standard UN bodies: cabinets, boards of directors, technical agencies, and committees set in the past. The premise is the rulebook; learn it or lose to someone who did.

## What counts as specialized

Anything that is not a generic GA organ or the present-day Security Council: a national cabinet, a corporate board, a technical agency like ICAO, a regional organization, a commission with invented powers, or any committee set at a historical moment. The circuit's major conferences lean on these heavily. McMUN runs boards of directors and agencies like ICAO alongside its GAs; NCSC's whole identity is realistic cabinets and councils with a modern-era norm.

These rooms are usually 15 to 50 seats, which means dais visibility is high and the GA mass-coalition game does not apply. What replaces country policy is the committee's own constitution: its powers, its procedures, and your specific portfolio inside it.

## The first rule: powers before positions

In a GA you research what your country wants. In a specialized body you must first research what this body can actually do. Before writing a word of position prep, answer:

- What instruments does this committee control? Budget, regulation, military force, corporate capital, treaty authority?
- What can it not do, and who else holds that power? A central bank cabinet cannot pass criminal law; a board cannot command an army.
- What does your individual seat control? Specialized rooms often assign portfolios (finance minister, chief engineer, regional director) and the delegate who uses their portfolio powers precisely will outplay the delegate giving generic speeches.

Delegates who propose actions outside the body's powers mark themselves as unprepared in the first session. Conversely, the delegate who says "this is exactly the kind of decision our charter lets us make, and here is the mechanism" becomes the room's procedural authority immediately.

## Technical committees: the homework premium

Agencies like ICAO, WHO-style bodies, or financial commissions reward a kind of prep most delegates skip: learning the actual technical domain. You do not need an engineering degree. You need the twenty key terms, the three real-world controversies in the field, and one or two real precedents the body has set. In a 40-person ICAO room, perhaps five delegates will have read about how the organization actually sets standards. Those five sound like adults in the room and the dais gavels from among them. Budget a full evening of prep purely on the domain, separate from your seat's position.

## Historical committees: play the moment, not the future

For committees set in the past, the cardinal sin is hindsight. You know how the story ended; your character does not. Directors consistently punish delegates who steer by foreknowledge and reward delegates who reason convincingly from inside the moment.

Prep for a historical room has three layers:

1. The timeline. Know the six months before the committee's start date in detail, and the major actors and pressures of the era in outline.
2. Your character. Their actual job, resources, relationships, rivalries, and what they personally knew and wanted at that date. Primary-source color (a real quote, a real decision they made) is gold in speeches.
3. The plausible counterfactual space. The committee will diverge from real history almost immediately. Your prep should make you fast at answering "what would this person plausibly do in a situation that never happened," which means understanding their incentives, not memorizing their biography.

One warning: check the conference's culture. NCSC explicitly avoids pre-WWII settings and prizes realism; other conferences happily run ancient or early-modern premises with looser realism norms. Match your flair level to the house style.

## Engaging the premise is the meta-skill

Every specialized room has delegates who quietly fight the premise: they wanted a normal committee and it shows. They generalize, they ignore portfolio powers, they treat the creative frame as decoration. They never win. The delegates who win lean in completely: they speak in the institution's vocabulary, propose actions through its actual mechanisms, and treat the invented stakes as real. Dais teams build these rooms because they love the premise. Loving it back, visibly and substantively, is the single most reliable differentiator.

## Queen's prep checklist for any specialized or historical seat

- Read the background guide twice; the second read is only for powers, procedures, and your portfolio.
- Write a one-page committee constitution in your own words: what this body can do, how it decides, what your seat controls.
- Do the domain homework: twenty terms, three controversies, two precedents.
- For historical rooms: one-page character brief with date-stamped knowledge limits.
- Prepare three actions you could propose in session one that only your seat could propose. That specificity is what the dais remembers.
