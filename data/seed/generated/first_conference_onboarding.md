# Your First Conference: An Honest Onboarding Guide

What a first travel conference actually feels like, what to pack, how to manage nerves, and what a realistic win looks like for a novice delegate. Read this the week before you leave.

## The honest version of what is coming

Your first conference will be overwhelming, and that is the design, not a failure. You will sit in a room where some delegates have done fifteen of these. They will speak in fluent procedure, form blocs with people they already know from the circuit, and look impossibly comfortable. Here is the secret every one of them knows: their first conference felt exactly like yours is about to feel. Nobody is born knowing what a motion to divide the question is. The gap between you and them is reps, nothing else, and this weekend is your first rep.

You are not expected to win anything. You are expected to come back better, with stories, and wanting to go again.

## What to pack

Clothes: western business attire for every committee session. That means a suit or equivalent, and the practical advice is two or three rotations so a coffee spill on Friday does not ruin Saturday. Comfortable dress shoes matter more than stylish ones; you will stand for hours in unmods. Pack one warmer layer because hotel committee rooms run either freezing or tropical with no middle setting. For winter conferences like McMUN, real boots and a real coat for the walk between venues.

Gear: laptop and charger, a portable battery, a notepad and two pens (unmods move too fast for typing sometimes, and some committees restrict laptops), your country research in printed form, water bottle, and snacks. Granola bars have saved more delegates than any procedural motion. Bring your student ID, any registration confirmations, and a small umbrella.

Health: whatever keeps you functional. Painkillers, throat lozenges (you will talk more in three days than in a normal month), earplugs for hotel sleeping, and any medication you take, packed in carry-on logic even on a bus trip.

## Schedule reality

A conference weekend is a marathon disguised as a series of sprints. Expect roughly: arrival and opening ceremonies Thursday, then committee sessions that run in blocks of two to three hours, several blocks a day, through Sunday morning. Add delegate socials on Friday and Saturday nights. Total sleep across the weekend will be less than you want.

Three rules keep novices functional:

1. Eat real meals. Skipping lunch to keep negotiating is a veteran move with veteran costs; as a novice it just makes your 4 p.m. session miserable.
2. Sleep is a competitive choice. The socials are genuinely fun and part of the experience; go. But leaving an hour before your roommates is invisible to everyone else and transformative for your Saturday.
3. Use the breaks to reset, not to doomscroll. Ten minutes of quiet, water, and a snack between sessions does more than you would believe.

## Anxiety management

Almost everyone is nervous before their first speech. The delegates who look calm have a system, so borrow one:

- Speak early. The longer you wait for the perfect moment, the heavier the silence gets. Your first speech should happen in the first session, and its only job is to exist. Thirty seconds, state your country's basic position, sit down. Every speech after that is easier.
- Script your first two sentences only. Knowing exactly how you will start removes most podium fear. After two sentences, momentum carries you.
- Lower the stakes accurately. Nobody in that room will remember a shaky speech by dinner. They are all worried about their own performance.
- Use the unmod side door. If formal speaking terrifies you, the unmoderated caucus is where you can contribute conversationally: offer to take notes for a working paper, suggest one clause idea to a small group. Many novices find their footing there first, and that is a completely valid route in.
- Have a reset ritual for bad moments. A wrong motion or a fumbled answer feels enormous in the second it happens. Breathe, write one note about what you would do differently, and rejoin. Veterans make procedural mistakes every conference; they just recover faster.

## Realistic goals for conference one

Set process goals, not award goals:

- Speak at least once in every session, in any format.
- Be a signatory on at least one working paper, and contribute one real idea to it.
- Learn five delegates' names and where they are from. The circuit is small; this weekend's bloc-mate is next year's familiar face.
- Ask one experienced delegate from another school how they prepared. People love answering this.
- Write down, on the bus home, three things you will do differently next time. That note is the actual product of the weekend.

If you do those five things, your first conference was a success regardless of the awards ceremony. Verbal commendations and better come from stacking weekends like this one. Every delegate on this team who has placed at a major conference started with a first conference where they mostly watched, occasionally spoke, and went home hooked. The only way to fail this weekend is to hide.
