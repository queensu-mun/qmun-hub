# Surviving and Winning the Large GA (100 to 200+ Delegates)

How to be one of the ten names the dais actually knows in a giant General Assembly room, where the speakers list is a trap and the working paper is the whole game.

## The core problem

In a 200-person GA, the dais cannot track 200 people. They track maybe ten or twelve. Every tactic in this guide serves one objective: be on that list by the end of the first session and never leave it. The delegates who fail in big rooms are not bad speakers; they are invisible ones. They wait politely on the speakers list, give one fine speech on day two, and are never seen again.

## The math you are fighting

A speakers list with 200 names and 60-second speeches is over three hours of talking. You might formally speak twice all weekend. Meanwhile a room that size produces perhaps 8 to 12 awards. So formal speeches cannot be your channel. Your channels, in order of leverage, are: working paper leadership, unmoderated caucuses, moderated caucus speeches, and only then the speakers list.

## Hour one: claim territory

Before the first unmod ends, you need three things: a bloc identity, a handful of committed allies, and the dais having heard your country name attached to an idea.

- Motion early. The first moderated caucus topic frames the debate, and the delegate who proposes and gets it passed is instantly on the dais radar. Have a motion drafted before the session starts.
- Give your first speech in the first hour by any means available: moderated caucus, speakers list if it is somehow short, or a point that legitimately requires the floor. The content matters less than existing.
- In the first unmod, do not wander. Pick the geographic spot, plant your placard, and recruit. The unmod tactics guide covers the mechanics; in a giant room the difference is scale. You are not building a group of five, you are building the core of a bloc that needs 20-plus signatories.

## The working paper is the award

In large GAs, awards follow the dominant working paper with remarkable consistency. The delegate who architected it, named it, and managed its co-sponsor coalition is the frontrunner. So:

- Volunteer to hold the laptop. Whoever physically controls the document controls the paper. This is the single most reliable power move in a large GA.
- Structure beats prose. Lay out the section skeleton before anyone writes clauses, and other delegates will pour their content into your architecture. Their clauses, your paper.
- Name it and brand it. A memorable acronym gives the dais a handle, and every time anyone says the paper's name, they are advertising you. Our McMUN papers (SLAP, FARM to Table) are the house template.
- Delegate generously and credit publicly. Big-room chairs listen for who other delegates say did the work. Praising your co-authors by country name in speeches paradoxically concentrates credit on you, because you are visibly the hub.

## Merge season

By the middle session there will be three to five papers and the dais will push merges. This is the most dangerous and most decisive phase. Rules:

- Merge from strength, early. The first two papers to merge usually set the chassis everyone else must bolt onto.
- Protect your clause language. In a merge, fight to keep your operative clauses recognizably yours, because clause authorship is what the dais audits when deciding awards.
- Never bad-mouth a rival paper by name in formal debate. Recruit its co-sponsors instead. Blocs in big rooms are loose; signatories defect to whoever treats them as important.

## Staying visible in the long middle

The grind sessions on day two are where most of the room mentally checks out. That is your opening:

- Speak in every moderated caucus, even 45 seconds of synthesis. A short speech that maps the debate (here are the three real disagreements in this room) reads as leadership.
- Use note-passing strategically and sparingly for coordination, and assume the dais sees everything.
- Work the small delegations. Forty countries in that room have no bloc and no plan. Each one you fold in is a signature, a vote, and a delegate telling the dais you included them.

## Voting day composure

Amendments and voting procedure are the final exam. Know the thresholds cold, whip your bloc's votes before voting bloc opens, and take losses gracefully on the floor. Chairs frequently settle close award calls on who handled the chaos of voting day like an adult.

## Realistic targets

First large GA: survive, speak five-plus times across all channels, get your name on a surviving paper. Second: co-lead a bloc and hold it through the merge. Third: run the dominant paper and contend for the gavel. The room size never stops being intimidating; the delegates who win are the ones who treat the scale as the strategy rather than the obstacle.
