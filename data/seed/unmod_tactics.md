# Unmoderated Caucus Tactics

The unmoderated caucus is where conferences are won and lost. Most delegates treat it as a break. The delegates who win awards treat it as the main event.

## What unmod is actually for

Unmod is not a break. It is unstructured negotiation time. The committee suspends formal procedure so delegates can talk directly, form coalitions, draft working papers, and cut deals that would be impossible through formal debate.

Chairs award delegates who move the committee forward. Moving the committee forward happens in unmod. If you sit with your friends and wait for someone to come to you, you are not moving the committee forward.

## The first unmod: what to do in the first 20 minutes

**Your goal:** know who is in the room and where they stand.

Talk to five people before you sit down. Not your friends. Five strangers, preferably from different regional blocs. Your opener: "I'm [name], representing [country]. What's your read on the first speaker's bloc?" You are not pitching your position yet. You are gathering intelligence.

By the end of the first unmod, you should know:
- Who the natural leaders are (who has a crowd around them already)
- Who is a swing vote (countries that could go either way)
- Who is your natural ally (similar interests, similar bloc)
- Who is your natural opponent (opposed interests)

With this map in hand, your second unmod has direction.

## Approaching strangers without it being awkward

The awkward delegate is the one who has a scripted pitch and delivers it regardless of response. Don't be that delegate.

Good approach: "Hey, quick question — where does [country] stand on the verification mechanism? We're trying to figure out which way the G77 is going to split on that."

You are asking for their opinion, not selling yours. People love being asked their opinion. It also immediately puts you in the frame of someone who is thinking about bloc dynamics, which is where the real action is.

If they're not interested, they'll say "still figuring it out" and move on. That's fine. Move to the next person. You're looking for the conversations that flow, not the ones you have to force.

## Building a bloc

A bloc is a coalition of countries that will support the same working paper. You need signatories. Signatories come from relationships built in unmod.

**Start with your natural allies.** Countries in your regional group that share your interests. Easy first conversations — you're already aligned.

**Add ideological allies.** Countries from other regions that share your position on this specific issue. G77 and NAM overlap heavily on sovereignty questions. EU and JUSCANZ overlap on rule-of-law questions. Identify the cross-cutting alignment.

**Target the swing votes.** The most valuable delegate in any committee is the one who could go either way. The lead delegate from a mid-sized country who isn't firmly in either camp is your highest-priority conversation. Find out what they actually want, and figure out if you can give it to them.

**Make the ask explicit.** At some point, you have to say: "We're going to table a working paper. Will you sign on?" Vague encouragement is not a commitment. Get names on paper.

## How to handle someone trying to poach your bloc

Poaching is when a competing bloc tries to pull your signatories away from your working paper. It happens in every conference.

Signs you're being poached: your allies are suddenly "in a meeting" in unmod, or they start asking questions about a specific clause you included.

Defense: stay close to your key signatories during unmod. The lead delegate of another bloc is most dangerous when your allies are unaccompanied. Check in frequently. If you hear your allies are wavering, find out what they want and adjust your paper.

Offense: if you need signatories from a competing bloc, you don't poach the lead — you find the weaker links. The delegates who signed the other paper but whose countries' interests are better served by yours. Make that argument directly.

## Running a working paper group in unmod

Once you have a coalition, you need to produce a working paper. Unmod is when this gets drafted.

Divide clauses by expertise and interest. If you have ten signatories and six operative clauses, assign a country or pair to each clause. Don't write the whole paper yourself — you'll be exhausted and your coalition won't feel ownership.

Set a merge deadline. If you're working with a competing paper, the time to propose a merge is before the papers are tabled, not after. A pre-tabling merge produces a stronger paper with more signatories. A post-tabling merge is an admission that your paper was weaker.

Use a shared Google Doc or paper. Name the doc with the working paper letter (WP A, WP B) and share access. Chairs sometimes ask to see drafts before tabling.

## The quiet move: the side deal

Not everything happens in the main bloc. Sometimes the most powerful move in unmod is a two-person conversation in a corner.

Side deals: a specific promise to a specific delegate in exchange for their vote or signature. "If you sign our paper, we'll accept your language on the verification clause" is a side deal. They are completely legitimate and extremely effective.

The best side deals are about operative clauses. Find out what language the swing vote needs in the resolution to justify signing, and give it to them. You'll know it worked when they stop circling the room and come back to you.

## Reading the room toward the end of unmod

As unmod closes, check:

- Do you have enough signatories to table? (Usually 20% of committee)
- Is there a competing paper with more support than yours?
- Is a merge viable or is the competition too far apart on substance?
- Have you flagged your paper's strongest clause to the chairs? (Chairs sometimes ask delegates what they're most proud of — have an answer)

The last five minutes of unmod before tabling is the highest-leverage five minutes of the conference. Use them.
