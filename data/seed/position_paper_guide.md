# Position Papers

A position paper is a pre-conference document that states your country's stance on the committee topic before debate begins. Most conferences require it; some use it for awards; all chairs read it. A good position paper does real work before you walk into the room.

## What a position paper actually is

It is not a research essay. It is not a summary of the background guide. It is a declaration of position — where your country stands, why it stands there, and what it will push for in committee.

A chair reading your position paper before conference should be able to answer three questions:
1. What is this country's core interest on this topic?
2. What prior commitments, treaties, or policies does this country hold?
3. What kind of resolution language would this country support or block?

If your paper does not answer those questions, it is not a position paper. It is a background guide excerpt.

## Standard format

Most conferences expect 250-500 words per topic. Some have templates; follow them exactly. If there is no template:

**Header**: Committee name, topic, country, delegate name, conference.

**Paragraph 1: Country context and stake**
What is your country's relationship to this issue? Is your country a directly affected party, a major donor, a regional bloc leader, an opponent of the proposed framework? Establish this in two to three sentences. Do not summarize the global problem — the chair knows the problem. Tell the chair who your country is in relation to it.

**Paragraph 2: Prior commitments and current position**
What treaties has your country signed? What votes has your country cast at the UN on related resolutions? What is the current government's public position? This is your factual foundation. It also locks you into consistency — a position paper that says your country supports binding emissions targets cannot be contradicted in committee without consequences.

**Paragraph 3: Proposed direction**
What do you want the resolution to accomplish? Be specific. "This delegation supports comprehensive action" is not a position. "This delegation will advocate for a voluntary technology transfer fund administered through the UNFCCC, with particular attention to SIDS and LDC access" is a position. Name mechanisms, not values.

## The research you need before writing

1. **Your country's voting record**: find how your country voted on similar UN resolutions. The UN Digital Library and resolutions database are the sources. This tells you your baseline position.

2. **Your country's treaty status**: is your country a signatory to the relevant convention? Party to the protocol? This creates obligations and coalition opportunities.

3. **Your country's stated policy**: does your government have a published position on this issue? Foreign ministry websites, UN mission statements, and UPR submissions are useful.

4. **Your regional bloc's general direction**: G77, EU, JUSCANZ, Arab League — where is your group going? You should know before you write whether your position is aligned with or departing from your bloc.

## Common mistakes

**Writing about the topic instead of your country's position.** Half a page on the history of the Israeli-Palestinian conflict is not a position paper for the Israeli delegation — it is a Wikipedia summary. Tell me where Israel stands.

**Vague commitment language.** "The delegation believes that international cooperation is essential" commits you to nothing and tells the chair nothing. Avoid value statements that any country could have written.

**Ignoring your actual country.** Do not write the position you wish your country held. Write the position your country actually holds. If you are representing a country with a problematic record on human rights and the committee is Human Rights Council, that tension is your material — acknowledge it and explain how your country frames it.

**Missing the word limit.** Under the word limit signals a delegate who did not take the prep seriously. Over the word limit (without a reason) suggests the delegate cannot edit. Conferences do not give awards to delegates who cannot read instructions.

## Using the position paper in committee

Your position paper is not just a pre-conference submission — it is a prep tool. Writing it forces you to commit to positions before you get in the room. This is valuable.

When you are in formal debate, your position paper is your baseline. When you negotiate in unmod, your stated positions create expectations from other delegates who read your paper. If you shift positions dramatically from your paper, allied delegations will notice.

The delegates who write their position papers last-minute produce papers that are interchangeable. The delegates who write their position papers as a genuine research exercise show up to conference knowing who they are in the room.
