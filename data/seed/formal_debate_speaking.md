# Speaking in Formal Debate

Formal debate is the structured portion of a committee session where delegates make speeches, yield time, and respond to each other through motions. Most delegates are mediocre at it. That is the opportunity.

## The structure of a speech

Most committees run 90-second to 2-minute speaker slots. A well-constructed speech in that time has three parts:

**1. The hook (10 seconds)**: Do not start with "The delegate of [country] thanks the committee for this opportunity." That is eight wasted words. Open with your strongest claim, a striking statistic, or a sharp question. "Forty-seven million people will be displaced by coastal flooding before 2050. This committee has no operative mechanism to address any of them."

**2. The substance (60-70 seconds)**: One argument, made well. Not three thin arguments. The most common mistake in formal debate is trying to say too much. Say one thing clearly, with evidence, and land it.

**3. The pivot (15-20 seconds)**: Close with your country's position and a forward-facing statement that connects to the bloc you are building. "The delegation of Bangladesh will be introducing working paper language on this point and invites like-minded delegations to collaborate during unmoderated caucus."

## Yielding time

When your speech ends early, you have three yield options:

- **Yield to another delegate**: pass remaining time to a specific ally. Use this to give an aligned delegate more floor time than the speakers list would otherwise grant.
- **Yield to questions**: open your remaining time to questions from the floor. Use this when you are confident on the substance and want to demonstrate it. Avoid yielding to questions on your first speech.
- **Yield to the chair**: the safest option. Remaining time returns to the committee. Use this when you have nothing to add.

Do not yield to questions unless you have at least 30 seconds remaining. A 10-second yield to questions is just creating an awkward moment.

## Handling questions after a yield

When another delegate questions you, they get a limited time (typically 30 seconds). You get a response window. The structure:

1. Listen to the full question before responding.
2. Reframe if needed: "The delegate asks about the enforcement mechanism. The delegation would note that clause 3(b) of our working paper addresses this directly."
3. Do not answer a hostile question on its own terms. Redirect to your strongest ground.

If the question is genuinely hostile and you do not have a good answer, say: "The delegation appreciates the question and looks forward to discussing this further in unmod." That is not a dodge — it is appropriate when the answer requires nuance the format does not allow.

## Right of reply

A right of reply is a motion to respond to something said in a previous speech that you believe misrepresented your country's position or was personally offensive. Most conferences allow one right of reply per speaker, at the chair's discretion.

Use a right of reply when:
- A delegate misquoted your country's position and you need the record corrected
- A delegate made a characterization of your government that is factually false

Do not use a right of reply to continue an argument you lost. Chairs will notice. The right of reply is meant for factual corrections, not debate extensions.

When you get a right of reply, be precise. State exactly what was said, state why it is incorrect, and state your country's actual position. Keep it under 30 seconds. End.

## Points

**Point of order**: raised when you believe the chair or another delegate has violated the rules of procedure. Example: a delegate speaks past their allotted time. Always a legitimate use. Do not use it to interrupt a speech you dislike — that is abuse of the point and chairs will cut you off.

**Point of personal privilege**: raised when something is affecting your ability to participate (can't hear, can't see, room is too cold). Legitimate but use sparingly. "The delegate requests the chair to ask speakers to use the microphone" is appropriate.

**Point of inquiry**: a question for the chair about procedure. "Would the chair clarify whether this committee requires a two-thirds majority for closure of debate?" This is not an interruption — it is directed to the chair, not to the speaker.

Points of order and personal privilege may interrupt a speaker. A point of inquiry may not.

## Making the speakers list work for you

The speakers list is an opportunity, not a formality. Strategies:

**Get on the list early, in the first unmod**: being one of the first five speakers sets the initial frame for the debate. Chairs weight early speeches more heavily because they shape the direction of the committee.

**Cluster with allies**: if your bloc signs up for consecutive slots, you can build an argument across speeches. The first speaker raises the problem; the second provides evidence; the third introduces your proposed solution. Choreograph it in unmod.

**Save a late-list slot for response**: put one of your bloc members at the back of the speakers list to respond to the other bloc's arguments after they are fully articulated.

## What chairs are watching

Chairs score formal debate on whether a delegate is moving the committee forward — not on vocabulary or speaking speed. Signs of a high-scoring speaker:

- References specific working paper language or prior speeches
- Builds on the previous speaker rather than ignoring them
- Makes an argument the committee had not considered
- Closes with something the committee can act on

Signs of a low-scoring speaker:

- States the problem without proposing anything
- Reads from a pre-written script that does not respond to what happened in committee
- Uses filler language ("the delegate believes that," "it is important to note")
- Runs exactly to time because they filled the time, not because they needed it

The delegates who win best delegate awards in formal debate are the ones who sound like they have actually been listening. Engage with what was said before you. Name other delegations' arguments and respond to them. The committee is a conversation, not a sequence of monologues.
