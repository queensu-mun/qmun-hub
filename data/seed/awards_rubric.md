# Awards Rubric (North American Collegiate Circuit)

Most chairs evaluate on roughly four axes, weighted differently by conference style. The exact mix matters more than the absolute score.

## What chairs evaluate

### 1. Substance (always heavily weighted)
- Knowledge of country's actual position
- Quality of operative clauses you push
- Accuracy of citations (real resolutions, not made up)
- Engagement with the technical content of the topic

### 2. Diplomacy / collaboration
- Bloc-building during unmoderated caucuses
- Willingness to merge papers and credit other delegates
- Respect in formal debate (does not interrupt or insult)
- Includes small / less-active delegations in coalition

### 3. Procedural skill
- Uses the right motion at the right moment
- Knows when to call for a moderated caucus to set framing
- Yields cleanly, asks pointed Points of Information
- Does not abuse procedure (chairs notice over-motioning)

### 4. Presence
- Confident speaking voice; direct eye contact
- Memorable opening speech (don't read from notes)
- Active in unmoderated caucuses
- Position paper quality (varies massively by conference)

## Conference-specific weighting

The mix of these four axes shifts from conference to conference, and from committee to committee within the same conference. Large GA committees tend to reward presence and the ability to stand out in a crowded room; small specialized agencies and crisis committees lean harder on substance and procedure; consensus-heavy circuits can penalize a delegate who reads as disruptive. There is no reliable published table for this. The only trustworthy read on how a specific conference grades is first-hand intel from delegates who have competed there. That intel lives in the alumni interviews in this archive, and it is worth far more than any general rubric. When you are prepping for a specific conference, search the archive for it and read what people who were in the room actually said.

## Award tiers

Most conferences award per committee:
1. **Best Delegate** (1 per committee) — peak across all four axes
2. **Outstanding Delegate** (1-2 per committee) — strong across most axes
3. **Honorable Mention** (1-2 per committee) — clear contributors who didn't win the room
4. **Verbal Commendation** (varies) — chair recognition for specific moments in committee, sometimes given out separate from the main awards.
5. **Best Position Paper** (sometimes separate) — purely on the written submission

## What loses awards

- Reading a memorized speech with zero eye contact
- Vague operative clauses ("encourage cooperation", "promote awareness")
- Hallucinating UN resolution numbers (chairs catch this)
- Bullying small delegations or refusing to credit signatories
- Ignoring chair instructions (e.g., disregarding a topic for moderated caucus)
- Procedural over-motioning to dominate floor time
- Late or weak position paper submission (where weighted)

## What gets remembered

- A merge negotiation you led that saved a dying working paper
- A crisis directive that fundamentally changed the arc
- A speech that landed an actual joke without alienating the room
- Naming a previously-silent small delegation as a sponsor (chairs love this)
- An impromptu solution that broke a deadlock chairs had given up on

## Calibration

Awards are inherently subjective. Chairs are college students grading college students. After two or three conferences, expect at least one award decision that surprises you. Per Russell Van Raalte's Art of MUN: "do not take defeat too seriously." Process > outcome.
